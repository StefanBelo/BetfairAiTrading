# Betting Strategy Ideas - Out of the Ordinary System

_Date: February 24, 2026_  
_Source: https://www.theukbettingforum.co.uk/XenForo/threads/out-of-the-ordinary-system.80034/_

This note recaps the "Out of the Ordinary System," a quirky selection method shared by user *Jackform* on the UK Betting Forum in October 2017. The thread chronicles the original rules, reader confusion, examples from UK and US cards, and several refinements posted over time.

---

## 1. Original Selection Rules

The core idea is to pick two horses from each race using only the **betting forecast** as printed in tip sheets. Form is reduced to two attributes: whether a horse won, placed (1st–4th) or was unplaced last time out (LTO). The forecast is read from shortest price to longest.

1. **Stage 1** – if the race contains a runner that won LTO: first choice is the *first LTO winner* seen; second choice is the *first LTO unplaced* horse.
2. **Stage 2** – if no LTO winners: first choice is the *first LTO unplaced*; second choice is the *first LTO placed*.
3. **Stage 3** – if no unplaced LTO: first choice is the *first LTO placed (1–4th)*; second choice is the *next LTO placed (1–4th)*.
4. **Stage 4** – if none of the above: first and second choices are simply the first two unplaced LTO horses found.

If any stage fails to produce a pair, the system restarts at stage 1 with the same race until a valid pair is identified (i.e. it cycles until two selections are available or the race is abandoned).

> *"This system is an old one from my notebook and can do well, although it is rather unusual."* – Jackform

## 2. Practical Notes & Clarifications

- A **betting forecast** means a published cloth or betting slate that ranks runners by market preference. Different publications sometimes give different forecasts, affecting the outcome.
- If a race lacks sufficient qualifying runners, the system may skip betting that race.
- The method is deliberately nonsensical – the author admits *"there ain't no logic to this and not for the sophisticated punter"* – and is viewed more as a pastime.
- Later posts show the system being applied to UK tracks (e.g., Chepstow, Yarmouth, Worcester, Leicester, Wetherby) and even American venues (Remington Park, Tampa Bay Downs) solely by following the same rules with early betting markets.
- Users reported staking **level stakes down the card** and stopping when a cumulative profit target was reached (e.g., 18 pts risked).
- For each meeting, real‑time results were posted; one early example produced nearly +6 points to level stakes.

## 3. Extended Rules & Constraints (May 2020)

In a later revision Jackform laid out additional constraints for practical use:

- **Race selection**: prefer cards with at least 8 runners if betting each‑way (3 places) and avoid handicaps with very short favourites (e.g., shortest price < 11/2 for EW). For win‑only betting on a paired selection the shortest price of the two should be at least 6/4.
- **Forecast reading**: always read left‑to‑right from the lowest forecast SP.
- **Four‑selection process** used to build the pair: if no clear pairing is found in the first process then move to the next, continuing until two horses are selected or none qualify.
- **Market sources**: selections could be derived from forecasts or early market odds (Racing Post early market around 08:40‑09:30). Different sources occasionally yield different pick pairs.
- **Exclusions**: horses with long breaks were to be removed from consideration.

## 4. Community Feedback & Performance Logs

- Initial readers struggled to decipher the vague description; subsequent posts clarified the steps and prompted tongue‑in‑cheek responses: *"I wish I had never posted this!"*.
- One poster expressed bewilderment at choosing an unplaced horse as second pick, highlighting the system’s arbitrary feel.
- A newbie member, *MaximDrew*, began posting daily selections (including US races) and logged results, showing modest profit and losses to illustrate how the mechanic worked across jurisdictions.
- The author emphasized the whimsical nature: *"when the fun stops – get serious and try something else!"*

## 5. Strategy Takeaways

- **Rule‑based selection** using minimal data (only LTO form and market order) is the essence of the system; it eschews deeper statistical analysis or horse‑specific traits.
- **Market timing** matters: selections were made using early morning odds or betting forecasts rather than late‑market prices.
- **Discipline**: bet each race meeting, use level staking, and cease when a modest profit target is reached or when the system feels stale.
- **Adaptability**: the method was applied to various race types (UK/American, handicaps/non‑handicaps) with the same recipe, stressing its arbitrary, exploratory character.

---

While the "Out of the Ordinary" method lacks intuitive justification or proven edge, it offers a charming example of how punters invent and share idiosyncratic systems. For serious strategy development, the key lessons are the importance of clearly defined rules, consistent application, and honest tracking of outcomes—even if the system itself is deliberately odd.
