# Betting Strategy Ideas - Big Odds Winners

_Date: February 24, 2026_  
_Source: https://www.theukbettingforum.co.uk/XenForo/threads/big-odds-winners.156769/_

This document summarises the key ideas, tactics and critiques that emerged from a UK Betting Forum thread titled **"Big Odds Winners"**. Users shared personal statistics, strategies, warnings, and analytic methods while debating whether backing longshots is a sensible approach.

---

## 1. The Core Thesis

- Favourites win roughly one third of races (≈32‑33%), which means about two thirds are *not* favourites. Some users concluded that backing bigger odds horses could therefore be more profitable or at least more entertaining.
- Posters such as **rbtblb82** tracked results and published raw counts of big‑odds winners and trebles from daily cards, arguing that the strike rate on horses priced ≥12/1 was around 13.5% (18.8% at ≥10/1) over 2,517 races.
- The idea is to capitalise on the sheer volume of horses available at long odds and the occasional large pay‑offs (e.g. 6,600/1 trebles).

## 2. Statistical Critiques & Fundamentals

Several experienced bettors pushed back with more rigorous statistical thinking:

- **Proportion matters.** Simply counting winners at big odds ignores how many horses at those odds were actually backed. A 30% strike rate on "big odds" is meaningless if you have to bet on the majority of the card to capture them.

- **Sample size and back‑fitting.** 49‑day samples are noisy; serious analysis uses multiple years with test/training/validation splits. Odds ranges are especially prone to over‑fitting.

- **Running metrics.** A meaningful comparison charts % of runners vs % of winners by odds band. For a full year of UK flat turf/AW, runners priced >10/1 accounted for 73% of starters but only 5–6% of winners. The further from par the odds are, the worse the proportion.

- **Odds‑to‑field‑size ratio (P/p ratio).** Convert both the advertised odds and the field size to probabilities (1/odds, 1/FS). A ratio ≥1.00 indicates the market price is as big or bigger than implied by field size. Over 2022 this metric showed that horses with ratio ≥1 made up 37.9% of runners but 72.2% of winners — a potential value indicator.

- **Jockey/trainer analysis.** Counting big‑odds winners by jockey without including runs is misleading. Trainer patterns ("nepotism" combos) are another route to uncover obscure value.

## 3. Tactics & Practical Advice

- **Value-based staking (MAP - Minimum Acceptable Price).** Start with a horse you want to back, then only bet if price meets your MAP. Odds become the *final* filtering criterion, not the starting point.

- **Use Betfair Starting Price (BFSP) rather than ISP/SP** for more realistic exit odds, especially when aiming at longshots.

- **Laying favourites** can be a complementary approach: if you doubt a short‑priced favourite, laying it may yield better edge than blindly backing longshots.

- **Selectivity & discipline.** Be prepared for long losing streaks; backing 10/1+ horses should be done with strong rules. Avoid betting on every longshot; excessive permutations (doubles/trebles) quickly blow the bank.

- **Beware market noise.** Big odds often arise from drift unrelated to actual chance; bookmakers' early prices and supply/demand dynamics create occasional mispricings.

- **Specialised systems.** Some users mentioned strategies like:
  - Following horses with weight drops/claimers.
  - Focusing on upside‑down handicaps (lightest weights first ridden by claimers).
  - Looking for horses with family connections (trainer/owner/jockey nepotism).

- **Lay combinational bets**: use the big‑odds pool to perm selections, but note the enormous number of possible bets makes this unprofitable in absence of strong edge.

## 4. Key Cautions

- **Back‑fitting**: Choosing odds ranges after results are known yields inflated success rates.
- **Ignoring runners**: A strategy that only counts winners without factoring in how many bets you'd have placed will not survive long term.
- **High variance**: Longshots come with long losing runs and require large bankrolls or small stakes.
- **Market efficiency**: Bookmakers and Betfair prices already embed public statistics; only subtle, exploitable inefficiencies remain.

## 5. Takeaways for Strategy Development

1. Conduct rigorous data analysis over multi‑year samples, ensuring runners versus winners comparisons.
2. Use probabilistic metrics (P/p ratio, field size adjustments) to find potential value horses.
3. Maintain strict staking rules (e.g., MAP) and resist the impulse to bet all available longshots.
4. Explore trainer/jockey patterns and other non‑price factors (weight drops, claimers) as filters for big odds.
5. Consider complementary approaches such as laying questionable favourites or seeking value in market drift rather than simply chasing high odds.
