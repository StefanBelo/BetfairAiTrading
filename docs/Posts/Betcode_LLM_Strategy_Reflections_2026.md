# Reflections on LLMs for Strategy Development: A 2026 Perspective

**Summary of Discussion**

Paul and Lorenzo recently shared their experiences and evolving perspectives on using large language models (LLMs) like Anthropic's Sonnet 4.6 and Opus for strategy development and coding. The conversation highlights the rapid progress in LLM capabilities, especially over the past year, and offers a nuanced view of their strengths, limitations, and the changing nature of software development.

## Main Topics

### 1. LLM Progress and Experimentation
- LLMs have improved significantly in the last 6–24 months, especially in reasoning and code generation.
- Paul’s structured experiment with Sonnet 4.6 showed that models now handle research-augmented generation (RAG) and concept explanation much better than before.
- LLMs can now identify gaps, suggest relevant research, and provide more meaningful analogies.

### 2. Practical Use and Limitations
- LLMs are valuable accelerators for those who understand their domain, but not a magic solution for building profitable strategies from scratch.
- Code generation is improving, but still requires expert oversight—blind reliance leads to errors and potential security issues.
- The best results come from iterative prompting, uploading relevant papers, and asking for explanations and edge cases.

### 3. Productivity and Developer Roles
- Some top engineers now write almost no code by hand, leveraging LLMs for rapid prototyping and research.
- The skillset for effective use is shifting: prompt engineering and domain expertise are more important than ever.
- The chaos in software development persists, but LLMs are making even weaker developers more productive in some cases.

### 4. Risks, Economics, and the Future
- Over-reliance on LLMs is risky; not using them at all is also a missed opportunity.
- The economics of LLMs are still uncertain—current heavy subsidies may not last, and real compute costs could change the landscape.
- The real value is in the middle ground: using LLMs as accelerators, not replacements.

### 5. Shifting Attitudes
- Paul’s stance has shifted from strong skepticism to cautious optimism, especially for domain research and synthesis.
- LLMs are not yet replacements for expert developers, but they are becoming essential tools for accelerating expert workflows.
- For non-experts, the risks remain high—expert oversight is still crucial.

## My Thoughts

This conversation captures the current reality for many working with LLMs: rapid progress, but no silver bullets. The most effective users are those who combine domain knowledge, critical thinking, and prompt engineering. LLMs are best seen as powerful research and productivity tools, not autonomous agents. The future will likely see further integration of LLMs into workflows, but the need for human expertise and judgment will remain central—at least until the next step change in AI capabilities or economics.

**Key takeaway:** Use LLMs to accelerate your work, not to replace your expertise. The trajectory is promising, but caution and critical thinking are still required.
